# Chapter 4 – Scientific Foundations (MacKenzie)
Source: Human-Computer Interaction: An Empirical Research Perspective, Chapter 4 :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}

---

# 1. What Is Research?

MacKenzie presents three definitions of research, moving from weak to strong.

## 1.1 Three Definitions of Research

### (1) Careful or diligent search
- Simply looking for something.
- Example: Searching files on a computer.
- Too weak for academic/scientific research.
- No theory building or hypothesis testing.

### (2) Collecting information about a subject
- Example in HCI: Observing users and counting errors.
- Descriptive.
- May not involve experimentation or theory revision.

### (3) Investigation or experimentation aimed at:
- Discovery of facts
- Interpretation of facts
- Revision of accepted theories or laws

This third definition aligns with scientific HCI research.

Key components:
- Experimentation
- Hypotheses
- Evidence
- Theory refinement

Important principle:
In HCI research, we do not "prove" things. We gather and test evidence.

---

# 1.2 Theory vs Law

## Theory
Two meanings:
1. A hypothesis proposed for investigation.
2. A confirmed explanatory framework after repeated empirical support.

Theories explain phenomena.

## Law
- More rigid and formal.
- Describes invariable relationships under given conditions.
- Rare in HCI due to human variability.

Example: Fitts’ Law
- Originally a model of human motor behavior.
- Predicts time for pointing tasks.
- Called a “law” due to generality and predictive power.

Important insight:
HCI relies more on models than strict laws.

---

# 1.3 Research Must Be Published

Publication:
- Final step of research.
- Must be peer-reviewed.
- Becomes part of archived knowledge.

Peer review evaluates:
- Novelty
- Methodology
- Evidence quality
- Contribution to field

If research is not published, it does not extend scientific knowledge.

Patents count as publication because they publicly disclose ideas.

---

# 1.4 Citations, References, and Impact

Citations:
- Connect research papers.
- Show intellectual lineage.
- Support claims.

Citation count:
- Proxy for impact.
- Highly cited work likely influential.

H-index:
Definition:
A researcher has H-index = n if they have n papers with at least n citations each.

Measures:
- Productivity
- Impact

Limitations:
- Favors senior researchers.
- Field-dependent.

---

# 1.5 Research Must Be Reproducible

Reproducibility:
- Others must be able to replicate the experiment.
- Requires detailed methodology.

Reviewers often ask:
Is the work replicable?

Standardized methodology supports replication and cumulative science.

---

# 1.6 Research vs Engineering vs Design

## Engineers/Designers
- Build products.
- Balance form and function.
- Work under deadlines.
- Focus on complete systems.

## Researchers
- Explore small, incremental ideas.
- Build early prototypes.
- Ask narrow, testable questions.
- Not product-driven.

Timeline:
Research → Engineering → Design → Product

Example:
Multi-touch gestures existed decades before the iPhone.
Research provides raw materials for future innovation.

---

# 2. What Is Empirical Research?

Empirical = based on observation or experience.

Two important meanings:
1. Based on direct observation.
2. Capable of being verified or disproved by observation/experiment.

Empirical research must be:
- Observable
- Measurable
- Testable

---

# 3. Research Methods

Three main empirical methods:

---

# 3.1 Observational Method

Characteristics:
- Qualitative
- Real-world settings
- High relevance
- Lower precision

Examples:
- Interviews
- Field studies
- Think-aloud protocols
- Cultural probes

Focus:
- Why and how behavior occurs.
- Thoughts, feelings, attitudes.

Strength:
- Realism and ecological richness.

Weakness:
- Limited control.
- Hard to quantify precisely.

---

# 3.2 Experimental Method (Scientific Method)

Core idea:
- Conduct controlled experiments.
- Manipulate one variable.
- Measure response.

Key Terms:

Independent Variable (IV):
- Manipulated factor.
- Example: Interface type.

Dependent Variable (DV):
- Measured outcome.
- Example: Task completion time.

Requirements:
- At least two conditions.
- Comparison is essential.

Important distinction:
Usability evaluation ≠ controlled experiment.
A single-interface test is not experimental research.

Strength:
- Precision.
- Can establish cause-and-effect.

Weakness:
- Artificial environment.
- Reduced realism.

---

# 3.3 Correlational Method

Purpose:
- Examine relationships between variables.
- No manipulation.

Example:
Privacy settings vs personality traits.

Results:
- Circumstantial relationships.
- Not causal conclusions.

Balanced method:
- Moderate precision.
- Moderate relevance.

---

# 4. Observation and Measurement

Observation is foundation of empirical research.

Observers:
1. Human observer
2. Apparatus (computer logging)

Challenges:
- Some behaviors hard to detect (e.g., gaze shifts).
- May require additional equipment.

Measurement transforms anecdote into empirical evidence.

---

# 4.1 Measurement Scales

Four scales:

---

## Nominal (Categorical)

- Labels only.
- No order.
- Example: Gender (M/F), Phone use (yes/no).
- Only counting is meaningful.

Used for:
- Group comparisons.

---

## Ordinal

- Ranked data.
- Order exists.
- Intervals unequal.

Example:
Preference rankings.

Cannot compute meaningful mean.

---

## Interval

- Equal spacing between values.
- No true zero.

Example:
Likert scales.
Temperature (Celsius).

Mean can be computed.

Limitation:
Equal spacing assumption debated.

---

## Ratio (Most Powerful)

- Equal intervals.
- True zero.
- Full mathematical operations allowed.

Common in HCI:
- Time
- Error rate
- Number of clicks
- Words per minute

Normalization:
Convert counts into rates for comparability.

Example:
2 errors in 50 characters → 4% error rate.

MacKenzie strongly emphasizes normalization for meaningful comparison.

---

# 5. Research Questions

Early-stage questions:
- Is it viable?
- Is it better?
- What are its strengths?

These are not testable.

Good research questions:
- Specify measurable variable.
- Specify comparison.
- Specify condition.

Example progression:
Bad:
Is the technique any good?

Better:
Is it faster than QSK?

Best:
Is entry speed (wpm) higher than QSK after one hour of use?

A testable question must be:
- Observable
- Quantifiable
- Comparative

---

# 6. Internal and External Validity

Crucial concepts.

---

## Internal Validity

Definition:
Extent to which observed effect is due to manipulated variable.

Threats:
- Participant variability
- Environmental differences
- Distractions

High internal validity:
Strong cause-and-effect confidence.

---

## External Validity

Definition:
Extent to which results generalize to other people and situations.

Threats:
- Unrepresentative sample
- Artificial lab conditions
- Unrealistic tasks

---

## Tradeoff

Improving external validity often reduces internal validity.

Example:
Real audience in experiment increases realism but introduces distraction.

Research design is a compromise.

---

## Ecological Validity

Refers specifically to realism of:
- Materials
- Tasks
- Environment

Closely related to external validity.

---

# 7. Comparative Evaluations

MacKenzie argues strongly for comparative studies.

Controlled experiments require:
- Independent variable with at least two levels.

Baseline condition:
- Established design included for comparison.
- Validates methodology.
- Enables cross-study comparison.

Research shows:
Participants interacting with multiple interfaces:
- Are more critical.
- Identify more problems.
- Provide more realistic ratings.

Single-interface evaluations provide weaker research insight.

---

# 8. Circumstantial vs Causal Relationships

---

## Circumstantial Relationship

- Found in correlational research.
- Variables associated.
- Cannot claim cause.

Example:
Smoking and cancer in correlational study.

---

## Causal Relationship

Requires:
- Controlled experiment.
- Random assignment.
- Manipulated independent variable.

Only experimental method can justify cause-and-effect claims.

Causal conclusions are powerful and central to HCI research.

---

# Big Themes of Chapter 4

1. HCI research must be empirical and measurable.
2. Controlled comparison is essential.
3. Cause-and-effect requires experimental control.
4. Measurement scale determines analysis power.
5. Internal and external validity are in tension.
6. Research must be reproducible and published.
7. Research is incremental and foundational to innovation.

# Survey Research in HCI – Scientific Foundation Notes

## 1. What Is Survey Research in HCI?

A **survey** is a method of gathering information by asking questions to a subset of people (sample) in order to generalize findings to a larger target **population**.

In HCI, surveys are primarily used to measure:
- Attitudes
- Intentions
- Experiences
- Awareness
- Task success
- User characteristics (demographic, technographic, psychographic)
- Comparisons across time, segments, geographies, or product versions

### Key Limitation
Surveys:
- Do NOT allow observation of context
- Do NOT allow follow-up probing
- Are weak at uncovering deep motivations or usability breakdowns

They are strongest for **measurement and quantification**, not explanation.

---

## 2. Historical & Intellectual Foundations

### Early Foundations
- Ancient censuses → population measurement.
- 19th century political polling → prediction and sentiment measurement.
- Francis Galton → questionnaires in psychology (nature vs nurture).
- Development of **psychometrics** (measurement of psychological constructs).

### Sampling Crisis
- 1936 Literary Digest failure
- 1948 Gallup failure  
  → Led to development of **random sampling** as gold standard.

### Key Intellectual Contributors
- **Thurstone & Likert** → attitude scaling.
- **Stanley Payne (1951)** → question wording research.
- **Tourangeau (1984)** → Four cognitive steps in answering survey questions:
    1. Comprehension
    2. Retrieval
    3. Judgment
    4. Mapping onto response
- **Groves (1989)** → Four types of survey error:
    - Coverage error
    - Sampling error
    - Measurement error
    - Nonresponse error
- **Couper (2008)** → Web survey visual design bias.
- **Krosnick & Fabrigar** → Scale reliability and questionnaire design.

### Standardized HCI Questionnaires
- SUS (System Usability Scale)
- SUMI
- QUIS
- NASA TLX
- CSUQ
- VisAwi

These operationalize constructs like usability, workload, satisfaction, and aesthetics.

---

## 3. When Surveys Are Appropriate

Surveys are appropriate when the goal is:

### A. Measuring Attitudes
Example:
- Customer satisfaction
- Perceived speed
- Perceived usefulness

Surveys allow benchmarking and longitudinal tracking.

### B. Measuring Intent
Online intercept surveys can capture intent during actual use.

Limitation: Context may still be missing.

### C. Measuring Task Success (Quantitatively)
Users can self-report success or difficulty.

BUT:
Does not explain *why* failures happen.

### D. Collecting User Experience Feedback
Open-ended responses help identify common frustrations or benefits.

### E. Characterizing Users
- Demographics
- Tech savviness
- Privacy orientation
- Openness to change

### F. Awareness Measurement
Determine whether low usage is due to:
- Lack of awareness
- Usability problems

### G. Comparisons
- Across time
- Across user segments
- Across experimental conditions
- Across competing applications

---

## 4. When NOT to Use Surveys

### 1. Precise Behaviors
Use log data instead.
Self-report is inaccurate for detailed behavior.

### 2. Underlying Motivations
Use:
- Ethnography
- Contextual inquiry
- In-depth interviews

People often don’t know their subconscious motivations.

### 3. Usability Evaluation
Surveys cannot uncover:
- Confusion points
- Missteps
- Interface misunderstandings

Use usability studies instead.

---

## 5. Combining Surveys with Other Methods

Surveys can:
- Follow qualitative research (to quantify findings)
- Precede qualitative research (to identify patterns)
- Complement A/B testing
- Be merged with log data
- Be combined with physiological data (EEG, facial EMG, etc.)

Important concept: **Triangulation**.

---

## 6. Research Goals & Constructs

Before writing questions:

### Step 1: Define Research Goal
Example: “Measure user happiness with Google Search.”

### Step 2: Identify Constructs
Construct = a unidimensional latent attribute.

Example:
- Overall satisfaction
- Perceived speed
- Perceived utility

Constructs are:
- Not directly observable
- Measured through multiple questions

### Validating Constructs
- Factor analysis
- Correlation/regression
- Cognitive pretesting

Avoid:
- “Nice-to-know” questions (increase fatigue)
- Overlong surveys

---

## 7. Population & Sampling

### Key Terms

- **Population** → Entire group of interest.
- **Sampling frame** → List of reachable people.
- **Sample** → Those invited.
- **Respondents** → Those who answer.

Mismatch between population and sampling frame → **Coverage error**.

---

## 8. Probability vs Non-Probability Sampling

### Probability Sampling (Gold Standard)
- Equal, nonzero chance of selection.
- Minimizes sampling bias.
- Enables generalization.

Examples:
- Random digit dialing
- Address-based sampling
- Intercept surveys with randomization
- Probability panels

### Non-Probability Sampling
- Convenience samples
- Volunteer panels
- Snowball sampling
- Self-selection

Risk: High sampling bias.

Often used in:
- University samples
- Hard-to-reach populations

---

## 9. Sample Size & Statistical Precision

Two critical parameters:

### Margin of Error
Range around estimate.
Common: 3%–5%.

### Confidence Level
Probability true value lies in interval.
Common: 95%.

For large populations:
- ~384 respondents → ±5% at 95% confidence.

Important:
Population size above ~20,000 does not significantly increase required sample.

Also must account for:
- Response rate
- Incidence rate of subgroups

---

## 10. Questionnaire Design

Two categories:

### Open-Ended Questions
Used when:
- Answer universe unknown
- Measuring natural quantities
- Exploring frustrations

Strength:
Rich qualitative insight.

Weakness:
Harder to analyze.

### Closed-Ended Questions
Used when:
- Answer universe known
- Measuring ratings or categories

Types:
1. Single-choice
2. Multiple-choice
3. Ranking
4. Rating scales


### Ratings

1. Unipolar construct: 0 -> 5
   - Used for importance, usefulness, interest and relative frequency
2. Bipolar construct: -3 -> +3 with midpoint
   - Used for satisfaction, ease of use, perceive speed and visual appeal

---

## 11. Rating Scales

### Unipolar Constructs
Range: 0 → extreme amount.
Examples:
- Importance
- Usefulness

Use:
5-point scale

### Bipolar Constructs
Range: Negative → Positive with midpoint.
Examples:
- Satisfaction
- Ease of use
- Speed

Use:
7-point scale

Include midpoint:
Research shows midpoint increases reliability and does not increase shortcutting.

---

## 12. Major Questionnaire Biases

### 1. Satisficing
Respondents use minimal cognitive effort.

Weak satisficing:
Incomplete processing.

Strong satisficing:
Random answers.

Causes:
- Long surveys
- Complex questions
- Low motivation

Mitigation:
- Short surveys
- Avoid repeated identical scales
- Avoid “no opinion” shortcut options
- Use trap questions

---

### 2. Acquiescence Bias
Tendency to agree with statements regardless of content.

More likely when:
- Agree/disagree format
- Low cognitive ability
- Deference to authority

Mitigation:
- Avoid agree/disagree statements
- Use construct-specific wording
- Reverse-keyed items

---

### 3. Social Desirability Bias
Respondents answer to look good.

Common in:
- Voting
- Religion
- Illegal acts
- Sexual behavior

Mitigation:
- Anonymity
- Self-administered surveys

---

### 4. Response Order Bias
Primacy or recency effect.

Mitigation:
- Randomize order
- Reverse scale direction between respondents

---

### 5. Question Order Bias
Earlier questions prime later ones.

Mitigation:
- Funnel approach (broad → specific)
- Easy questions first
- Sensitive questions last
- Group related questions

---

## 13. Questions to Avoid

- Broad questions
- Leading questions
- Double-barreled questions
- Recall-heavy questions
- Prediction questions
- Hypothetical questions
- Feature prioritization questions

Why?
They introduce measurement error and unreliable responses.

---

## 14. Visual Design Effects

Visual design affects measurement:

- Uneven spacing biases responses.
- Larger text boxes → more text.
- Drop-down menus → slower, more error-prone.
- Contextual images can frame responses.
- Progress bars:
    - Good for short surveys
    - Harmful for long ones

Visual design can create **measurement bias**.

---

## 15. Pretesting

### Cognitive Pretesting
Think-aloud method.
Checks:
- Interpretation
- Missing options
- Construct validity

### Field Testing
Pilot with subset of sample.
Check:
- Break-off rates
- Completion time
- Open-ended response quality

Iterate before full launch.

---

## 16. Paradata & Response Metrics

Paradata = data about survey process.

Important metrics:
- Click-through rate
- Completion rate
- Response rate
- Break-off rate
- Completion time

Online surveys:
- 30–40% email response typical
- 15% or lower for intercept surveys

---

## 17. Increasing Response Rates

Based on Dillman:

- Multiple contacts
- Personalization
- Explain importance
- Assure confidentiality
- Use incentives

Incentives:
- Monetary > non-monetary
- Non-contingent often stronger than contingent

---

## 18. Data Cleaning

Remove:
- Duplicate responses
- Speeders
- Straight-liners
- Fraudulent responses

Decide:
- Listwise deletion
- Pairwise deletion
- Partial removal

Low-quality data → threatens validity.

# Data Analysis and Reporting (Expanded Notes)

This stage begins **after all survey responses have been collected**.  
It transforms raw survey data into meaningful, valid insights.

The chapter divides this into five major parts:

1. Data Preparation and Cleaning
2. Analysis of Closed-Ended Responses
3. Analysis of Open-Ended Comments
4. Assessing Representativeness
5. Reporting Survey Findings

---

# 1. Data Preparation and Cleaning

Before analysis, the dataset must be cleaned to remove low-quality responses.

Why?  
Because poor-quality responses introduce **measurement error** and threaten validity.

### Key Terms (Simple Definitions)

- **Duplicate responses** → Same person completes survey multiple times.
- **Speeders** → Respondents who finish unrealistically fast.
- **Straight-liners** → People who select the same rating for many questions without thinking.
- **Fraudulent responses** → Fake or bot-generated entries.
- **Listwise deletion** → Removing the entire respondent from analysis.
- **Pairwise deletion** → Removing only specific missing responses.

### What Researchers Look For

- Extremely short completion times.
- Identical answer patterns.
- Failed trap questions.
- Gibberish open-ended responses.
- Missing data patterns.

Important concept from the chapter:
Data cleaning decisions must be made carefully because removing data changes the effective sample size :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}.

---

# 2. Analysis of Closed-Ended Responses

Closed-ended questions produce quantitative (numeric) data.

### Common Types of Analysis

### A. Descriptive Statistics
Used to summarize data.

- **Mean** → Average value.
- **Median** → Middle value.
- **Mode** → Most common response.
- **Standard deviation** → How spread out the answers are.
- **Percentage / Proportion** → Share of respondents selecting an option.

Example:
“60% of users report being satisfied.”

---

### B. Confidence Intervals

- **Confidence level** → Probability that estimate falls within range (e.g., 95%).
- **Margin of error** → Range around estimate (e.g., ±5%).

Example:
60% ± 5% means true population value likely between 55–65%.

---

### C. Comparing Groups

Researchers may compare:
- Geographic segments
- User types
- Experimental vs control groups

Common tools:
- **t-test** → Compares two group means.
- **ANOVA** → Compares more than two groups.
- **Chi-square test** → Tests differences in proportions.

---

### D. Correlation and Regression

Used when studying relationships.

- **Correlation** → Measures strength of relationship between two variables.
- **Regression** → Predicts one variable from others.

Example:
Does usage frequency predict satisfaction?

---

### E. Construct Validation

If measuring abstract concepts (constructs):

- **Factor analysis** → Identifies underlying dimensions in multiple questions.
- **Reliability (Cronbach’s alpha)** → Measures internal consistency of scale.

Simple definition:
Reliability = How consistently items measure the same concept.

---

# 3. Analysis of Open-Ended Comments

Open-ended responses produce qualitative data.

### Steps

### A. Coding

- **Coding** → Categorizing responses into themes.
- **Codebook** → Document defining each category.
- **Inter-rater reliability** → Agreement between coders.

Example:
Responses grouped into:
- “Battery issues”
- “Slow performance”
- “Confusing UI”

---

### B. Thematic Analysis

- **Theme** → Recurring pattern in responses.
- **Frequency count** → Number of times a theme appears.

Important:
Frequency helps prioritize issues.

---

### C. Sentiment Analysis (Optional)

- **Sentiment** → Positive, neutral, or negative tone.
- Can be manual or automated.

Open-ended responses provide:
- Context
- Explanations
- Nuance

But:
They are harder to generalize statistically.

---

# 4. Assessing Representativeness

Critical step before generalizing results.

Key question:
Does the sample reflect the population?

### A. Comparing Sample to Population

Researchers compare:
- Age
- Gender
- Geography
- Usage patterns

If sample differs → potential bias.

---

### Key Terms

- **Representativeness** → Degree to which sample mirrors population.
- **Weighting** → Statistical adjustment to correct imbalances.
- **Coverage error** → Some population groups missing from sampling frame.
- **Nonresponse bias** → Those who respond differ from those who don’t.

Example:
If young users are overrepresented, results may skew toward their attitudes.

---

### Weighting (Simple Explanation)

Weighting = Giving underrepresented groups more influence in analysis.

Example:
If older users are 20% of population but only 10% of sample, their responses may be weighted more heavily.

Important:
Weighting can adjust proportions but cannot fix severe sampling frame problems.

---

# 5. Reporting Survey Findings

Final stage: Communicating insights clearly and responsibly.

Good reporting includes:

### A. Transparency

Report:
- Sampling method
- Sample size
- Response rate
- Margin of error
- Limitations

---

### B. Clear Visualization

- Bar charts for proportions
- Line graphs for trends
- Tables for comparisons

Avoid:
- Distorting scales
- Overstating small differences

---

### C. Interpretation vs Description

Descriptive:
“65% reported satisfaction.”

Interpretation:
“Satisfaction increased significantly after the redesign.”

Avoid causal claims unless experimental design supports it.

---

### D. Acknowledge Limitations

Important to mention:
- Coverage error
- Nonresponse bias
- Measurement error
- Self-report limitations

Scientific reporting requires honesty about uncertainty.

---

# Big-Picture Structure

Data Analysis & Reporting is about:

1. Cleaning bad data
2. Summarizing and comparing numeric results
3. Extracting meaning from text responses
4. Ensuring results are representative
5. Communicating findings responsibly

# 2. Early Computing (1940s–1970s)

## Key Idea: Computers were NOT for ordinary people.

- Located in guarded, air-conditioned rooms.
- Used only by engineers and computer scientists.
- Interaction required typing commands.
- No concern for usability.

**Definition (simple):**
A text-based interface where users type commands.

Example:
- Unix commands like `grep`, `awk`
- User must memorize syntax.

### Key Problem:
- Requires recall (remembering commands).
- No visibility of system state.
- Easy to make errors.
- Designed for experts, not everyday users.

This sets up why HCI became necessary.

# 3. Human Factors → HCI

## Human Factors (Ergonomics)

**Definition:**
A field studying human capabilities, limitations, and performance to design efficient and safe systems.

HCI is:
> Human factors applied specifically to computer systems.

### HCI is interdisciplinary:
- Psychology (especially cognitive psychology)
- Sociology
- Anthropology
- Computer science
- Linguistics

## Why Important?

Bush predicted ideas similar to:
- Hypertext

- Bookmarks
- Linked information systems

## Memex

**Definition:**
A hypothetical device for storing and linking information through associative indexing.

### Associative Indexing

**Definition:**
Linking related pieces of information so selecting one automatically brings up another.

Sounds like:

### Big Idea:
Information overload is a problem.

# 5. Ivan Sutherland – Sketchpad (1962)

## Why Important?

Sketchpad was:
- A graphical system.
- Used a light pen.
- Allowed users to manipulate objects directly.

## Direct Manipulation (later defined by Ben Shneiderman)

**Definition:**
Interaction style where users interact directly with visible objects rather than typing commands.

Characteristics:
- Visible objects
- Incremental action
- Rapid feedback
- Reversible actions
- Replace language with action

Example:
Dragging shapes instead of typing commands.

### Why Revolutionary?

Before Sketchpad:
Users typed instructions.

With Sketchpad:
Users *manipulated graphics directly*.

This marks the beginning of modern GUI interaction.

# 6. Douglas Engelbart – The Mouse (1963)

## Why Important?

The mouse:
- Enabled point-and-click interaction.
- Allowed indirect control of on-screen cursor.
- Became central to GUI systems.

### Key Concept: Point-Select (Point-and-Click)

**Definition:**
Move cursor to object and press button to select.

## First Empirical User Study in HCI (English et al., 1967)

Compared:
- Mouse
- Light pen
- Joystick (absolute and rate control)
- Knee-controlled lever
- Grafacon

Measured:
- Access time (time to reach device)
- Motion time (time to move cursor)
- Error rate

### Independent Variable

**Definition:**
What the experimenter changes.
Here: input device type.

### Dependent Variables

**Definition:**
What is measured.
Here: time and error rate.

### Counterbalancing

**Definition:**
Varying order of conditions to prevent order effects.

### Why This Study Matters

- Controlled experiment.
- Clear variables.
- Replicable.
- Foundation of empirical HCI research.

Essay angle:
This marks the shift from invention to scientific evaluation.

## First Commercial GUI System

Included:
- Windows
- Icons
- Menus
- Pointing device (WIMP)

### WIMP

**Definition:**
Windows, Icons, Menus, Pointing device.

Still basis of modern GUIs.

## Desktop Metaphor

**Definition:**
Using familiar office objects (folders, documents, trash) to structure digital interface.

Why powerful?
- Uses existing knowledge.
- Reduces learning curve.

## Direct Manipulation vs Command Line

### Command Line:
- Sequential programming.
- System controls order.
- User types commands.
- Requires recall.

### Direct Manipulation:
- Event-driven programming.
- User controls order.
- Uses visible controls.
- Requires recognition.

---

## Event-Driven Programming

**Definition:**
Software model where system responds to user events (clicks, actions) in any order.

More complex than sequential programming.

---

- Very expensive ($16,000).
- Closed architecture.
- Not truly personal computing.
- Could not run external software.

Important insight:
Great design ≠ commercial success.

You can estimate task time before building system.

HCI moved from “trial and error” to predictive modeling.

# Chapter 1 Notes – The Psychopathology of Everyday Things
(Don Norman)

---

# 1. Central Thesis of the Chapter

The main argument of Chapter 1 is:

When people have trouble using everyday objects, the problem is usually bad design — not user incompetence.

Norman argues that:
- Designers often blame users for “human error.”
- In reality, many errors are design-induced.
- Good design should make correct use obvious and incorrect use difficult.

The chapter introduces core principles of interaction that explain why some products are intuitive and others are frustrating.

---

# 2. The “Norman Door” Problem

Norman begins with doors as an example of poor design.

Common experience:
- Pushing when you should pull.
- Pulling when you should push.
- Not knowing if a door slides.

Why does this happen?

Because the design does not communicate:
- What actions are possible.
- Where to act.
- How to act.

Key lesson:
If a simple object needs a sign that says “PUSH,” the design has failed.

Good design should not require trial and error for simple tasks.

---

# 3. Discoverability and Understanding

These are two key characteristics of good design.

## Discoverability

Definition: The ability to figure out what actions are possible and how to perform them.

A product has good discoverability if:
- Controls are visible.
- Actions are obvious.
- Users do not need instructions.

Failure of discoverability leads to:
- Trial and error.
- Frustration.
- Mistakes.

Example:
A flat metal plate on a door suggests pushing.
A vertical handle suggests pulling.

---

## Understanding

Definition: Knowing what a product does and how it works.

Many products fail because:
- They have too many controls.
- Their controls are unclear.
- Users memorize instead of understand.

Norman argues:
Simple objects should not require manuals.
Complex systems require good explanation and training.

---

# 4. Human Error vs. Design Error

Norman challenges the idea of “human error.”

Example: Three Mile Island nuclear accident.
Operators were blamed.
Investigation showed:
- Control panels were poorly designed.
- Error was almost inevitable.

Important principle:
Design must assume humans will make mistakes.

Humans:
- Are imprecise.
- Are distracted.
- Rely on patterns and habit.
- Do not always read instructions.

Machines:
- Follow strict logic.
- Have no flexibility.
- Require precision.

Therefore:
Design should accommodate human behavior, not expect perfect logic.

---

# 5. Human-Centered Design (HCD)

Definition:
A design philosophy that begins with understanding human needs, abilities, and limitations.

Main principles:
- Observe real users.
- Test prototypes.
- Iterate repeatedly.
- Focus especially on failure cases.

Key insight:
Designing for when everything works is easy.
Designing for when things go wrong is critical.

HCD ensures products match how people actually behave.

---

# 6. Fundamental Principles of Interaction

Norman introduces six major principles:

1. Affordances
2. Signifiers
3. Constraints
4. Mapping
5. Feedback
6. Conceptual Models

These principles explain how people understand and use objects.

---

# 7. Affordances

Definition:
An affordance is the relationship between an object and a user that determines what actions are possible.

Important:
Affordances are relationships, not properties.

They depend on:
- The physical characteristics of the object.
- The capabilities of the user.

Examples:
- A chair affords sitting.
- A button affords pushing.
- A knob affords turning.
- A handle affords pulling.

Affordances exist even if they are not visible.

---

## Perceived Affordances

Definition:
Affordances that are visible and recognizable.

Example:
A handle that clearly looks graspable.

If affordances are not perceivable, users struggle.

---

## Anti-Affordances

Definition:
Features that prevent certain actions.

Example:
- A wall prevents walking through.
- A locked door prevents entry.

Anti-affordances are important in controlling behavior.

---

# 8. Signifiers

Definition:
A signifier is a signal that communicates where and how to act.

Affordances determine what is possible.
Signifiers tell users what to do.

Examples:
- A “PUSH” label.
- An arrow icon.
- A flashing light.
- A sound.
- A handle’s shape.

Norman introduced the term “signifier” because designers misused “affordance.”

Important distinction:
Affordance = possibility.
Signifier = communication.

---

## Accidental Signifiers

Signifiers that were not intentionally designed but still communicate meaning.

Examples:
- A worn path in grass suggests where to walk.
- Trash on a ledge suggests it’s acceptable to place items there.
- A bookmark shows how much of a book remains.

---

## Misleading Signifiers

When appearance suggests the wrong action.

Example:
A sliding door with a pull-style handle.

This creates confusion and errors.

---

# 9. Mapping

Definition:
Mapping is the relationship between a control and its effect.

Good mapping makes actions intuitive.

Example:
Turn steering wheel right → car turns right.

Natural mapping:
Uses spatial relationships.

Examples:
- Move control up → object goes up.
- Light switches arranged in same layout as lights.
- Seat-shaped car controls that move seat in same direction.

Poor mapping:
Digital watch with multiple unlabeled buttons.

When mapping is unclear:
- Users must memorize.
- Errors increase.
- Learning becomes difficult.

---

# 10. Feedback

Definition:
Feedback is information returned to the user about what action has occurred.

Without feedback:
- Users repeat actions.
- Users become anxious.
- Users do not know if system responded.

Examples:
- Elevator button lights up.
- Sound after pressing a key.

---

## Good Feedback Must Be:

- Immediate (delays cause confusion)
- Informative (not just random beeps)
- Appropriate in intensity
- Prioritized (important alerts stand out)
- Not excessive

Too little feedback → confusion.
Too much feedback → overload and alarm fatigue.
Bad feedback → dangerous (e.g., hospitals, airplanes).

---

# 11. Conceptual Models (Most Important Concept)

Definition:
A conceptual model is a simplified explanation of how something works.

Users build conceptual models from:
- What they see.
- Experience.
- Instructions.
- Prior knowledge.

Conceptual models allow users to:
- Predict outcomes.
- Understand errors.
- Recover from problems.

---

## Mental Models

Definition:
The conceptual models inside the user’s mind.

Mental models:
- May be incomplete.
- May be incorrect.
- Guide behavior.

---

## Refrigerator Example

Two controls:
- “Freezer”
- “Refrigerator”

Users assume:
Each control independently adjusts its own compartment.

Reality:
- Only one cooling system.
- Controls interact.
- 24-hour delay before seeing effect.

The design suggests a false conceptual model.

Result:
Confusion and frustration.

Lesson:
If the conceptual model implied by the design is wrong, users cannot succeed.

---

# 12. System Image

Definition:
The system image is all information available to the user about the product.

Includes:
- Physical structure.
- Labels.
- Manuals.
- Advertisements.
- Website.
- Help systems.

The system image shapes the user’s mental model.

---

## Designer–System–User Triangle

Three components:

1. Designer’s conceptual model.
2. System image.
3. User’s mental model.

Designers want the user’s model to match theirs.
But communication only happens through the system image.

If system image is unclear or misleading → mismatch occurs.

Good communication is essential for good design.

---

# 13. The Paradox of Technology

As technology improves:
- More features are added.
- Complexity increases.
- Usability often decreases.

Example:
Old mechanical watches:
- One knob.
- Clear mapping.
- Few functions.

Modern digital watches:
- Many buttons.
- Multiple modes.
- Hidden functions.
- Hard to remember.

More power = more design challenges.

This is the paradox:
Technology makes things possible,
but design must make them usable.

---

# 14. Major Essay Themes to Prepare

Be ready to discuss:

- Why users should not be blamed for design failures.
- The difference between affordances and signifiers.
- Why conceptual models are critical.
- The importance of mapping and feedback.
- Human-centered design and its principles.
- The role of the system image.
- The paradox of technological complexity.
- Why poor feedback can be dangerous.
- How design must assume human error.
- Why good design reduces need for instructions.

---

# 15. Key Terms with Simple Definitions

Affordance: A possible action between a user and an object.

Perceived affordance: An affordance that is visible and understandable.

Signifier: A signal that tells users what action to take.

Mapping: The relationship between a control and its effect.

Feedback: Information returned to the user after an action.

Conceptual model: A simplified explanation of how something works.

Mental model: The user’s internal understanding of a system.

System image: Everything visible that communicates how the system works.

Human-centered design: Designing based on human needs and limitations.

Anti-affordance: A feature that prevents certain actions.

Discoverability: The ability to figure out what actions are possible.

Understanding: Knowing how and why something works.

# Chapter 5 – The CHI of Teaching Online
David Joyner

## Big Picture / Central Argument

**Core Thesis:**  
In fully online education, the *classroom itself becomes a user interface*. Because of this, **user interface (UI) design principles and learning design principles overlap and blur together**.

Traditionally:
- UI design → focuses on usability and smooth interaction.
- Learning design → focuses on long-term learning outcomes.

In online education:
- The interface *is* the classroom.
- Design decisions affect both usability AND learning outcomes simultaneously.

This chapter performs a **case study** of an online graduate-level HCI course and applies major HCI principles to evaluate its learning design.

# Important Foundational Concepts

## 1. Fully Online Learning Environments

The chapter emphasizes a shift:

- Traditional classroom → UI supplements learning
- Online classroom → UI *is* learning

Key difference:
- Synchronous environments try to recreate physical classrooms.
- Asynchronous environments must redesign interaction entirely.

### Jargon

**Synchronous**  
Activities happening in real time (e.g., live Zoom lecture).

**Asynchronous**  
Activities done at different times (e.g., recorded lectures, forum posts).

---

# 1. Flexibility

### Definition (Universal Design Principle)
"The design accommodates a wide range of individual preferences and abilities."

### In UI:
Allow users to tailor interactions.

### In Learning:
Allow students flexibility in:
- Where they learn
- When they learn
- How they engage

---

## Types of Flexibility in the Course

### A. Geographic Flexibility

Students can participate from anywhere.

Why it matters:
- Removes requirement to relocate
- Removes barriers unrelated to course content (e.g., commuting ability)

Key idea:
If walking/driving is not essential to computer science, it should not be required.

---

### B. Temporal Flexibility

Students can work whenever they want.

Important tension:
- Too much flexibility → students disengage.
- Too little flexibility → excludes working professionals.

Solution:
- Asynchronous lectures
- Forums instead of live discussion
- Exams open for several days

---

### C. Preference Flexibility

Students choose:
- Re-watch lectures
- Work ahead
- Participate via forum OR peer review OR feedback

Important insight:
Mandatory participation can create gender or communication bias.
Multiple pathways reduce this.

---

# 2. Equity

### Definition
"The design is useful and accessible to people with diverse abilities."

### Sub-guidelines:
- Provide same means of use whenever possible.
- Avoid segregating or stigmatizing users.

---

## Equity Through Flexibility

Flexibility allows:
- Working professionals
- Caregivers
- People with disabilities
- People with chronic illness

to participate.

Key insight:
Equity is not only about classroom experience — it is about who gets access in the first place.

---

## Equity Through Admissions

Traditional program:
- Limited capacity
- Accepts only top few percent

Online program:
- No fixed capacity
- Anyone meeting minimum qualifications admitted

Effect:
Expands access to students who are capable but not elite.

---

## Equity Through Anonymity

Online students control how much identity they reveal.

Why important?

### Jargon

**Stereotype Threat**  
When awareness of a negative stereotype about one’s group harms performance.

Online format reduces:
- Visual cues
- First-impression bias
- Gender/race stereotypes

Students with:
- Disabilities
- Speech impediments
- Obesity
- Transgender identity
- Behavioral disorders

may experience greater equity.

---

# 3. Consistency

### Definition (Norman, Nielsen, Constantine)
Consistent systems reduce cognitive load and help users predict outcomes.

### In UI:
Buttons and actions behave predictably.

### In Learning:
Expectations are predictable.

---

## A. Assignment Cadence

Problem:
Online lectures are permanent — no forcing function.

Solution:
Weekly required submissions.

Purpose:
Recreate rhythm of in-person lectures.

Effect:
Students know:
- Every week → something due
- Every Sunday → deadline

Reduces uncertainty.

---

## B. Announcement Cadence

Weekly Monday + Friday announcements.

Purpose:
- Push information
- Prevent students from constantly checking site

Important finding:
Perfect automation reduced “human feel.”
Consistency must not remove perceived instructor presence.

---

## C. Administrative Consistency

- All deadlines: Sunday 11:59 PM UTC-12 (“Anywhere on Earth” time)
- No random midweek deadlines (except peer review incentive)
- Time-gated elements open at predictable times

Why?

Students don’t waste mental energy checking due dates daily.

---

# 4. Distributed Cognition

### Definition
Cognitive tasks can be distributed across people and tools.

Instead of all thinking happening inside the brain, some thinking happens:
- In tools
- In documentation
- In community
- In environment

---

## A. Offloading Through Announcements

Traditional class = instructor pushes info.
Online class = student must pull info.

Push model reduces cognitive load.

Without push:
Students spend energy worrying about missing something.

---

## B. Offloading Through Documentation + Forums

Complete documentation is available.

Not every student reads all of it.

But collectively:
Someone always knows the answer.

Knowledge is distributed across community.

---

## C. Offloading Through Assessment Design

Exams are:
- Open-book
- Open-note
- Open-video
- Open-forum

Why?

Assessment evaluates:
Student + tools + environment.

Not pure memorization.

This reflects real-world distributed cognition.

---

# 5. Structure

### Definition (Constantine & Lockwood)
Design should reflect underlying structure.

Online affordance:
Pre-produced videos can align with logical structure rather than fixed lecture time.

Also:
Course schedule can be redesigned without being constrained by room bookings.

---

# 6. Perceptibility

### Definition (Nielsen)
System keeps users informed about status.

In education:
Persistent gradebook access.

Students can pull information about their progress.

Important:
Immediate grade visibility increases clarity.

---

# 7. Tolerance

### Definition (Universal Design)
Design minimizes consequences of accidental errors.

In course:
- 2-hour grace period after deadline
- Late submissions attachable before grading begins

Why important?

Large class → many honest mistakes.
System design reduces harsh penalty for small errors.

---

# 8. Feedback

### Definition (Norman)
Feedback must be:
- Immediate
- Informative
- Actionable

---

## Applications

1. Assembly-line grading workflow
    - Large scale → faster turnaround

    - 0.5 if late

Result:

Peer review increases rapid feedback cycle.
